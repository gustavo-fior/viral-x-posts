# Viral X Posts & Launch Videos

A playbook for writing X posts and short demo videos that actually get traction. Distilled from a founder who ran this playbook across multiple product launches.

## Core mental model

X is a **tech/developer audience**. If the ICP isn't technical, push the user toward LinkedIn instead — don't force X. What goes viral on X reaches LinkedIn ~2 weeks later, not the other way around.

Two separate problems, solved in order:
1. **Warm up the account** so posts have a baseline audience to land on
2. **Craft the viral post or launch video** using proven templates

A cold account (<500 followers) won't go viral no matter how good the post is — the algorithm needs a warmed-up account to amplify.

## Part 1: Warming up the account

Target: **500–1,000 followers minimum** before expecting any launch post to pop.

Tactics, in rough order of effectiveness:

- **Tag relevant communities when posting.** X has communities (like subreddits) — "Build in Public" is the default for founders. Always attach posts to a fitting community so members see and follow.
- **Tag famous accounts** in posts where it's contextually natural (e.g., a photo with them, a reply to their idea). Retweets from big accounts bring waves of followers.
- **Post every single day.** Non-negotiable. Consistency trains both the algorithm and the audience.
- **Post short, punchy, motivational/insight one-liners** that resonate with the target ICP. If targeting founders, post things founders nod at. No shame — repetition builds recognition.
- **Post your wins publicly.** Screenshots of first payments, first customers, first revenue. People root for visible underdogs and remember you.
- **Copy viral templates from competitors.** Search your space on X, find posts that popped recently, and reuse the *structure* (not the content) immediately. Trends decay fast — what worked last year is dead now.
- **Cross-pollinate from YouTube.** YouTube builds deep "OG fans"; X builds reach and brand. If the user has other channels, funnel that audience to X.
- **Accept that one random post will blow up** for dumb reasons. Keep posting daily until it happens. Realistic timeline: **several months to ~1 year** of daily posting before the breakout.

When advising, be honest about the timeline. Don't promise instant virality.

## Part 2: The launch / demo video formula

This is the highest-leverage format on X for product launches. Strict structure:

### Hard rules

- **Under 60 seconds total.** Over a minute = attention drops off. People are calibrated to 15-second reels.
- **One feature per video.** Not one click — one *pipeline* / one user journey / one thing the customer can do end-to-end. Save other features for future videos.
- **Show, don't explain.** Every second spent explaining what the product *is* instead of showing it *working* is a second losing viewers.

### The 60-second structure

| Time | What goes here |
|---|---|
| **0:00–0:01** | **Visual hook.** One second. Something weird, cute, loud, or unexpected that stops the scroll. Can be the founder on camera, a surprising visual, a domain name flash — anything that isn't a boring product screen. |
| **0:01–0:06** | **One-line product hook.** 3–7 words max. Examples: *"You can now give Claude a brain."* / *"Introducing [X], but better."* / *"The CRM for AI agents."* Paired with the product already visible on screen. |
| **0:06–0:10** | **Show the product working.** Not a feature tour — the actual pipeline in motion. If storytelling, the founder frames the problem ("I'm a founder, the day after launch, Slack is chaos, I can't tell what's happening") and *immediately* opens the product. |
| **0:10–0:55** | **Walk through the single pipeline.** Tight voiceover. Every sentence earns its place. |
| **0:55–1:00** | **Clear CTA / domain name.** |

### Storytelling variant

For products where the value isn't obvious from a UI alone, **the founder plays the ICP**. "Hi, I'm [name], I'm a [ICP], and today [specific pain]. So I opened [product]..." — this lets the demo double as a problem/solution narrative. Still bound by the 10-second rule: problem framed and product open within 10 seconds.

### Writing the script

The single biggest script mistake is filler words. Method that works:

1. Write the full script long-form, unfiltered.
2. Then cut aggressively. Delete every word that isn't load-bearing.
3. Read it out loud with a stopwatch. If it's over 55 seconds of spoken audio, cut more.

**Don't use Claude (or any LLM) to write the final script verbatim.** LLMs add filler and miss founder-voice nuance. Use them for brainstorming hooks or tightening specific lines, but the script should sound like the founder talking.

## Part 3: Post-format heuristics

When helping someone draft an X post (not a video):

- **Text posts build followers; video posts convert on launches.** Don't over-rely on video for daily posting — text is the workhorse.
- **Founder voice beats corporate voice.** Lowercase, casual, short sentences, honest framing. No "We're thrilled to announce..."
- **Hook in the first line.** X cuts off posts — the first line must make the rest worth expanding.
- **Screenshots > links.** GitHub repos, dashboards, visualizations, revenue numbers — devs click on things that look like things devs look at.
- **Engagement-gated offers work.** "Comment [keyword] and I'll DM you the [thing]" drives replies, which the algorithm rewards.
- **Match the current trend template.** Before writing, the user (or Claude) should scan 5–10 recent viral posts in the same space and mirror the structure.

## How to use this skill

When the user asks for help with an X post or launch video:

1. **Clarify the goal first:** launch video? daily growth post? big announcement? Each has a different template above.
2. **Ask for ICP and product in one line** if not already clear — everything downstream depends on it.
3. **For launch videos:** draft a 60-second script following the time table above. Mark timestamps. Keep the spoken script tight enough to actually fit.
4. **For posts:** draft 2–3 variants in founder voice, each with a distinct hook angle. Keep them short.
5. **Be honest about warm-up.** If the user has a cold account and expects a launch video to blow up, tell them it probably won't until the account is warmed up — and give them the Part 1 tactics.
6. **Avoid filler and LLM-ese.** Cut "thrilled," "excited," "game-changing," "revolutionize," "leverage," "unlock," em-dashes used for drama, and any sentence that could appear in a press release.
